I was born in 1975 in a quiet patch of northern New Mexico, where the sky always felt bigger than anything life could throw at you. My dad was a retired Air Force mechanic who had a thing for old planes, and by sixteen I was already taking lessons in a beat-up yellow Piper Cub he helped restore. But it was my mom who taught me to listen - to the wind through junipers, to the hush of a canyon at dusk. She was a park ranger, and I spent more hours than I can count wandering the high desert trails with her, learning to read the land like a story.

In my late twenties, I settled in Flagstaff, Arizona - close enough to the sky and the trails to feel right. I flew part-time for a small charter outfit and spent the rest of my days guiding nature walks through the parks nearby. It was during one of those weekends that I first tried kayaking, paddling the Salt River with a couple friends. Something clicked. The rhythm of water, the way it moved around stone and through silence - it reminded me of flying, but slower, closer. After that, I started exploring rivers and lakes all over the Southwest, just me, the boat, and whatever the current offered.

Now it’s 2025, and while I still spend most of my days flying, hiking, or paddling through the backcountry, there’s another side to the story folks around here like to whisper about. Somewhere along the way - maybe after I rescued that stranded biologist with only a kayak, a thermos of coffee, and a lucky coyote tooth - I sort of stumbled into legend. These days, they call me Cactus Matt, the unofficial guardian of the Southwest. There are tales of me lassoing a dust devil to save a runaway hot air balloon, or navigating a flash flood in the Gila with nothing but a tarp and an old harmonica. Are they true? Well, I won’t say they’re false. All I know is, people smile a little wider when they see the old Piper flying low over the canyons. And honestly? I like it that way. A quiet life with just enough legend to keep things interesting.
